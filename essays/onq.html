On Q

Whenever I'm asked why I began work on Q, I tend to say stuff like, "Oh, well certain aspects of quantum algorithms can be quite hard to code by hand in today's gate-based environment", and then go off on a whole explanation of oracles and classical functions, and then how Q provides a more user-friendly way to implement them.

Though that's not really why I decided to make it. Sure, it felt great to say that Q could do some things better than other languages, but adding state-of-the-art features was not a design goal.

I built Q because it seemed like an interesting thing to do. I like building things from the ground up, and I don't know why, but even the thought of extending software triggers unfavourable chemical responses in my brain. If someone were to hand over the entire codebase for say, Facebook, and asked me to manage it, then I can pretty much guarantee you'll see me working on a re-implementation. That's also why the option of extending QISKit or other frameworks seemed so bizarre. It's not that wasn't able to, it's just, I prefer building new things, and the notion of being able to build my own language seemed much more powerful than writing some library.

With Q I had complete creative freedom over the language, a blank canvas if you will. It allowed me to explore syntaxes, experiment with form, and also add a few last-minute Easter-eggs into the compiler:)
I certainly do love QISKit, and no doubt it's an engineering marvel, but my only qualm is that it's Python-based. I'm a C-person, and I really like curly braces, so in a way, Q was a way of remaking QISKit in the style of C.

Right now you might be thinking, okay, but why call it Q? Like, why the one letter that is literally used everywhere in the industry? Actually, at first it wasn't. For most of its development phase, it was referred to as Q++ since I was writing the compiler in C++. However, I realized that I could design an object-oriented version, and it seemed a lot more fitting to call that one "Q++".
Honestly though, I couldn't think of a name. I mean, what else was I meant to call it? A number of ideas came to mind (all rejected, for good reasons), "Qobra", (a rip-off of Python?) Or maybe QRust, and have the logo be a half-eaten slice of pizza? Eventually I settled on Q. Simple, succinct, and most importantly

I'm likely going to end up open-sourcing the compiler code, and that'll be when I have a decent piece written-up on the language itself. Until then, if anyone wants to try Q out, just email me at tambdeayush@gmail.com
