On Q

Whenever I get asked why I began work on Q, I tend to say stuff like, "Oh, well certain aspects of quantum algorithms can be quite hard to code by hand in today's gate-based environment", and then go off on a whole explanation of oracles and classical functions, and then how Q provides a more user-friendly way to implement them.

To be completely honest, though, that's not really why I ever decided to make it. Sure, it felt great to say that Q could do some things better than other languages, but it's not like I went, "Hey, I want to make it easier to code oracles. Let's spend the entire year trying to do that!".

No, the main reason is because it seemed like an interesting thing to do. I like building things from the ground up. I don't know why, but even the thought of extending software triggers unfavourable chemical responses up there. If someone were to hand over the entire codebase for say, Facebook, and asked me to manage it, then I can pretty much guarantee you'll see me working on a re-implementation. That's also why the option of extending QISKit seemed so bizarre. It's not that I didn't want to, it's just, I prefer building new things, and the notion of being able to build my own language seemed much more powerful than writing some library.

Also, with Q I had complete creative freedom, a blank canvas if you will. I could design a language as I saw fit, not just in terms of functionality, but aesthetically too. While the likes of Silq and Q# do seem pretty cool, I've never tried to use them and neither do I wish to. They might be really powerful, and no doubt have exquisite features that ensure rivals quake in their (binary) boots, but to me they just look ugly. Q isn't particularly an "objet d'art" either, but I much prefer its C-style nature. Design, however, is subjective, and many may prefer Q# or Silq. All I'm trying to say is, Q is to all those C aficionados out there.

That's not exactly publishable either. I can't just waltz in and say, "Look, I made a language that I believe is much more aesthetically pleasing" - not very scientific, is it? There's no metric to accurately measure intuitiveness and ease-of-use, because these things are generally non-uniform and vary from person-to-person. Rather, I have to play on Q's strengths and portray them as distinguishing features. For example, one of the main things I wanted in Q were arithmetic/logical operators. Turns out, the way Q implements these are much more compact than QISKit or Q#, so now the title of my report is:
"A Higher-Level Programming Language For Quantum Oracle Construction".

As a final note, I want to answer the question as to why name it Q? Like, why the one letter that is literally used everywhere in the industry? Actually, at first it wasn't meant to be. For most of its development phase, it was called...
< insert drumroll >
Q++ !
...since I was writing the compiler in C++. However, I realized that I could make an object-oriented version of the language, and it seemed a lot more fitting to call that one "Q++".

In all seriousness though, I couldn't think of a name, so I settled on Q. I mean, no-one has actually created a programming language with the same name. (I'm not counting KBD system's Q since it's only designed to be a thin interface on the K programming language)

I mean, what else was I meant to call it? "Qobra", a rip-off of Python? Or maybe QRust, and have the logo be a slice of pizza? Q was the only name that actually sounded good and wasn't too cringey.

I'm probably going to end up open-sourcing the compiler code, and that'll be when I have a decent piece written-up on the language itself. Until then, if anyone wants to try Q out, just email me at tambdeayush@gmail.com
